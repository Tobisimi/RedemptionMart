# RedemptionMart — Business Design & Go-to-Market Document

**Version:** 1.0
**Market:** Redemption City, Ogun State, Nigeria

---

## 1. The Problem

Redemption City is a large, self-contained community with numerous markets,
vendors, and service providers scattered across the city. Buyers who need
a product face a daily friction loop:

- They don't know which vendor sells what they need
- They physically walk or ride around to find out
- Prices are undisclosed until they arrive
- There is no record of what was bought, from whom, or for how much
- Disputes have no evidence trail

For sellers, the problem is the opposite: their reach is limited to whoever
physically passes by. There is no way to showcase products to the wider
community, no way to build a digital reputation, and no way to receive
orders from someone across the city.

WhatsApp groups exist as a workaround, but they are informal, unstructured,
and offer no payment protection or transaction record.

---

## 2. The Solution

RedemptionMart is a simple, safe way to buy and sell within Redemption City,
online.

It is a Progressive Web App — shareable via a link, no download required —
where:

- Sellers create a shop, list products with photos and prices, and receive
  orders on their phone
- Buyers browse, search, pay securely, and choose delivery or pickup
- Payments are held safely until the buyer confirms the item is correct —
  only then does the seller get paid
- Every transaction is recorded digitally

---

## 3. Target Users

**Buyers:** Residents of Redemption City who want to find and order products
without physically searching for them. Most will be familiar with WhatsApp
and basic smartphone use. They need trust and simplicity above all else.

**Sellers:** Market vendors, small businesses, and independent traders within
Redemption City who currently rely on physical foot traffic and informal
WhatsApp promotion. They need a free, low-risk way to expand their reach
within the community.

---

## 4. Business Model

**Free to join. We earn only when a transaction succeeds.**

| Revenue Stream | Detail |
|---|---|
| Transaction commission | 3% of every completed sale that goes through the platform's payment system |
| No listing fee | Sellers pay nothing to join or list products |
| No subscription | No monthly charges for buyers or sellers |

**Why this model:**
- Zero upfront cost removes the biggest barrier to seller adoption on a
  new platform
- 3% only on completed sales aligns the platform's incentive with the
  seller's success — we make money when they make money
- This rate is consistent with industry norms for hyperlocal marketplaces
  (reference: Alutamarket, a campus equivalent, operates at 3%)

**Future revenue levers (not V1):**
- Featured/promoted seller placements once buyer traffic justifies it
- Services marketplace listings (tailors, printers, cobblers, etc.)
- Potential B2B: bulk order facilitation for estates or organizations

---

## 5. Competitive Landscape

| Platform | Why it doesn't solve this |
|---|---|
| Jumia / Konga | National logistics, not Redemption City specific; no same-day local delivery |
| WhatsApp groups | No search, no payment protection, no transaction record |
| Instagram/Facebook | Discovery only, no ordering or payment rails |
| Word of mouth | Slow, unreliable, no digital trail |

RedemptionMart's moat is specificity: it is built exclusively for one
community, which means faster delivery, more relevant products, known
sellers, and community trust in a way no national platform can replicate.

---

## 6. Go-to-Market Strategy

**The core problem with two-sided marketplaces:** you need sellers before
buyers will come, and buyers before sellers will bother. The standard
mistake is launching to both sides simultaneously and getting traction
with neither.

**Our approach: supply-first, then demand.**

**Phase 1 — Founding Sellers (pre-launch)**
Identify 10–15 known, trusted, high-volume vendors within Redemption City
across key categories (food, fashion, household goods, electronics). Onboard
them personally — show them the platform, help them set up their shop, take
their photos if needed. Offer founding-seller incentive: free featured
placement for the first 3 months.

The goal of Phase 1 is not revenue. It is having enough real product
listings that when a buyer opens the app for the first time, they find
things they actually want.

**Phase 2 — Seeded Launch**
Launch to founding sellers' existing customers via WhatsApp and community
word-of-mouth. These buyers already trust these sellers — the platform
simply gives them a safer, more convenient way to transact with them.
No cold acquisition needed for the first cohort.

**Phase 3 — Community Growth**
Encourage sellers to share their product links on WhatsApp, church
bulletin boards, and community notice boards. Each shared link is a
distribution channel. The PWA format means any link click is a frictionless
onboarding — no app download required.

**Phase 4 — Organic Expansion**
As transaction volume grows and reputation builds, open seller onboarding
to new vendors. Introduce reviews publicly so new buyers can trust new
sellers. Introduce featured placement as a revenue lever once there is
enough buyer traffic to make visibility worth paying for.

## 7. Key Metrics to Track (V1)

- Number of active seller shops
- Number of products listed
- Number of orders placed per week
- Order completion rate (orders confirmed by buyer / orders placed)
- Dispute rate (disputes raised / completed orders)
- Platform GMV (total transaction value flowing through the app)
- Commission revenue

---

## 8. Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Sellers prefer cash and resist digital payments | Start with sellers already familiar with Paystack or bank transfers; offer onboarding support |
| Low buyer trust in a new platform | Escrow model (money held until buyer confirms) directly addresses this; reviews build trust over time |
| Sellers bypass platform after initial discovery (cash deals off-platform) | Commission only on platform-processed payments — no enforcement burden; value is in convenience and record-keeping |
| Delivery logistics not controlled by platform | V1 does not operate its own delivery fleet; sellers arrange delivery or buyers pick up; platform facilitates payment only |
| Low smartphone/data penetration in some seller demographics | PWA is lighter than a native app; will test on low-end Android devices common in the market |

---

## 9. Vision

RedemptionMart starts as a marketplace for products in Redemption City.

The longer-term vision is a full community commerce layer: physical products,
services (tailors, printers, repair shops), and eventually community
programs. Redemption City is large enough and self-contained enough to
sustain a dedicated platform — and being the first to own that digital
infrastructure creates durable community utility that larger platforms
cannot easily replicate.
